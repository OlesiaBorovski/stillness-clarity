Description (1 paragraph)

Stillness = Clarity is an interactive audiovisual piece in which cursor movement acts as perceptual interference. The system measures cursor velocity and distance from the screen center, mapping them to sound and visuals: movement increases sonic masking (noise + low-pass filtering) and destabilizes the orb (jitter, trails), while stillness restores brightness and triggers a soft “arrival” note. Pitch is quantized to a musical scale, and color fields shift with tension (distance) and clarity (stillness), inviting the user to experience a rule of perception: restraint produces resolution.

Perception • Gestalt (vision/hearing) • Multisensory integration • Illusions • Visual dominance
Perception is active organization: the brain groups, predicts, and stabilizes sensory input. This work uses movement as uncertainty: rapid motion increases acoustic masking (noise + reduced spectral clarity) and visual instability (jitter + smear), weakening figure/ground segregation; stillness restores stable grouping. Gestalt equivalences appear cross-modally: figure/ground (orb/tonal center vs field/noise), proximity/grouping (repeated scale tones and clustered particles), good continuation (smooth parameter change and continuous trails), and closure (a discrete “arrival” event). Multisensory integration is supported by a common-cause structure: the same latent variable (movement intensity) drives both modalities, and temporal synchrony promotes binding. The piece invites cross-modal bias (akin to audiovisual capture): users may attribute sonic causality to the visible orb because vision supplies strong spatial priors and stable scene explanations, often dominating audition in causal inference.
Semantic typologies & relationship with attention (examples)
The interaction carries layered semantics. Sensorimotor: a quickly learned rule (move → interference, still → clarity). Affective: stillness reads as presence/calm, while distance introduces tension via pitch and color. Conceptual: it becomes a metaphor for attentional regulation, where agitation produces noise and sustained attention produces resolution. Attention is shaped bottom-up by salient events (sparks, sudden stabilization, arrival note) and top-down by the user’s goal of achieving clarity, encouraging deliberate slowing and control.
Semiology: Icon • Index • Symbol • Gaver: nomic • metaphorical • symbolic mappings
The strongest sign type is indexical: jitter, blur, and added noise are direct traces of movement intensity. The orb and aurora field have partial iconicity (they resemble energy/atmosphere more than literal objects), while titles, labels, and scale names are symbolic conventions. In Gaver’s terms, movement-to-masking is nomic-like (resembling physical interference reducing clarity); distance-to-tension is metaphorical (space → harmonic affect); and the choice of musical scales is symbolic-cultural, relying on learned musical codes to shape meaning and expectation.
Attention • Intensity • Continuities and discontinuities (taxonomy)
Attention is modulated by intensity: not only loudness/brightness, but also rate-of-change and eventfulness. The piece alternates continuity and discontinuity. Continuities include smooth cursor drift producing continuous filter/pitch modulation, continuous trails, and slow hue drift (supporting sustained attention). Discontinuities include threshold-crossing into stillness triggering an “arrival” note (event boundary), spark punctuations, and abrupt motion producing immediate masking and instability (interrupt). This creates a legible attentional rhythm: continuity invites exploration; discontinuity captures attention and marks structure.
Paradigmatic examples: installation • sound art • audio-visual performance • experimental film
Installation paradigms: sustained audiovisual fields with punctuating events (ambient continuity + discrete markers) reflect how installations let attention drift and then re-focus at thresholds. Sound art paradigms: indexical motion→sound mappings align with interactive sound art where action leaves perceptual traces. Audio-visual performance paradigms: shared control signals creating audiovisual lock mirror realtime A/V strategies built on synchrony. Experimental film paradigms: discontinuity structuring attention (cuts, punctuation, phrase boundaries) is echoed by the arrival event acting as a micro-cut inside a continuous sensory field.
